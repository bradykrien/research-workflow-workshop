# Zotero
{: .fs-8 }

A free, open-source tool for collecting, organizing, and citing your research sources.
{: .fs-5 .fw-300 }

---

## What is It Good For?

Zotero provides a couple of useful features. First, it makes it easy to **collect, organize, and store** research materials like 
You're juggling PDFs in random folders, copying and pasting citation info into a Word doc, and reformatting your bibliography every time you submit to a different journal. Zotero gives you one place to store everything and handles citation formatting automatically.

**Where it fits in the workflow:** Gather → Organize → Write & Cite

---

## Get started (45-minute path)

### 1. Install Zotero (5 min)

- Go to [zotero.org/download](https://www.zotero.org/download/) and install **Zotero 7** (the desktop app)
- Install the **Zotero Connector** for your browser (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, or Safari)
- Create a free account at [zotero.org/user/register](https://www.zotero.org/user/register) — this enables syncing across devices

### 2. Explore the interface (5 min)

When you open Zotero, you'll see three panels:
- **Left panel:** Your library and collections (think of these like playlists — a source can live in multiple collections)
- **Middle panel:** The items in whatever collection you've selected
- **Right panel:** Details and metadata for the selected item

Take a moment to look around. Notice the toolbar at the top — you'll use the green "+" button and the magic wand icon shortly.

### 3. Add your first sources (10 min)

Try all three methods:

**Method A — Browser Connector (recommended for most things):**
Navigate to a journal article, book page, or library catalog entry in your browser. Click the Zotero Connector icon in your toolbar. Zotero grabs the citation data (and often the PDF) automatically.

**Method B — Magic Wand (ISBN/DOI/PMID):**
Click the magic wand icon in Zotero's toolbar, paste in a DOI or ISBN, and press Enter. Zotero looks up the metadata and creates an entry.

**Method C — Manual entry:**
Click the green "+" button, choose the item type (book, journal article, etc.), and fill in the fields. Use this as a last resort.

**Exercise:** Add 3–5 sources from your current project using at least two of the methods above.

### 4. Organize with collections and tags (10 min)

- **Create a collection:** Right-click "My Library" → "New Collection." Name it after your current project, a course, or a chapter.
- **Drag items** into your collection. Remember: this doesn't move them out of your main library — it's like a playlist.
- **Add tags:** Select an item, look for the "Tags" tab in the right panel. Add a few tags that make sense for your research (e.g., "methods," "primary source," "to-read").

**Exercise:** Create 2 collections and tag the sources you just added.

### 5. Cite in a document (10 min)

- Open a Word or Google Doc
- In Word: look for the "Zotero" tab in the ribbon. In Google Docs: go to Extensions → Zotero → show Zotero toolbar
- Click "Add/Edit Citation," choose a citation style (e.g., Chicago, MLA, APA), and search for one of your sources
- At the end of your document, click "Add/Edit Bibliography" to generate a formatted bibliography

**Exercise:** Create a test document with at least 2 in-text citations and a bibliography.

### 6. Set up syncing (5 min)

- Go to Zotero → Settings → Sync (on Mac: Zotero → Preferences → Sync)
- Sign in with the account you created in Step 1
- Check "Sync automatically"
- Note: free accounts include 300 MB of file storage. This is for PDFs and attachments — the citation metadata itself doesn't count toward the limit.

## Tips for long-term success

- **Use the Connector constantly.** Whenever you find a useful source, click the button. It takes 2 seconds and your future self will thank you.
- **Don't over-organize early.** A few broad collections and some tags is plenty to start. You can restructure later without losing anything.
- **Explore Zotero groups** for collaborative projects — everyone in a group shares a library.
